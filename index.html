Appearance

Eastern newts are colored yellow or greenish-brown with red spots bordered by black on their back. They have light yellow bellies with black spots. They have moist, rough skin.

Habitat

Eastern newts live in coniferous and deciduous forests. They thrive in moist or muddy environments. In their eft stage, they can travel far from their original home. Adult Eastern newts prefer muddy environments but are also seen moving to land in a dry spell.

Lifespan and Size

Eastern newts live between 12 to 15 years. They can grow between 2.5 to 5 inches long.

Life Cycle

Aquatic Larva

The Eastern newt larva has gills and stays in the pond environment where it was hatched. They are brown green and lose their gills once they change to the red eft.

Red Eft or Juvenile Stage

It takes around 3 months for a young Eastern newt to get from larva stage to the red eft stage. This stage is characterized by a bright orange-red color with darker shades of red spots in the back. The pattern of the spots can vary depending on the subspecies.

Aquatic Adult

This stage takes a period of 2 - 3 years. The eft will find a pond and transform into an aquatic adult. An adult is characterized by a dull olive green dorsally and a dull yellow belly. The black-rimmed red spots remains. The Eastern newt then develops bigger, blade-like tail and skimy skin.

Conservation Concerns

Eastern newts are increasingly threatened by climate change, habitat loss, presence of invasive species, over-exploitation and diseases.

Diet

Eastern newts' diet is varied. They can eat insects, springtails, soil mixes, small mollusks and crustaceans, amphibians, worms and frog eggs. Then can also consume snails, beetles, ants and mosquito larvae. Because of this diet, they help control insect population. Easter newts are active animals and needs to feed every two to three days.

Reproduction

They breed from late winter to early spring. Males use their tails to court females by movements, wiggling and emitting pheromones. They mate in an amplexus position. The male's sperm packet is deposited on the bottom of the body of water. The female then picks these up with her cloaca in order to fertilize her eggs. Around 200 to 400 jelly-covered eggs are laid on underwater plants. Eastern newts do not perform parental care of their young.

Survival

Eastern newts secrete toxins through their skin. This help shield them from predators. These toxins are harmful to humans thus Eastern newts shouldn't be held with bare hands. The bright red color of the adults also serve as a warning to predators. Eastern newts have poison glands producing tetrodotoxin. This toxin is known to cause paralysis, skin irritation and even death in predators.Because of their tail depth, Eastern newts are also capable of swimming quickly away from danger.
